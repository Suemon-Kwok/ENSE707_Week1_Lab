1. Who are the stakeholders for this small banking system?
Bank staff (users), customers (indirectly, via account correctness), the developer/team, and possibly a QA/compliance reviewer.

2. What does quality mean to each stakeholder?
Staff want ease of use and reliability; customers want correctness (right balance, no unexpected overdrafts); developers want maintainable, testable code; auditors/compliance want validation and traceability.

3. Which defects were detected through testing?
Overdraft allowed (withdrawing more than the balance), and negative amounts allowed (negative deposits, withdrawals, and opening balances all silently accepted).

4. Which defects could have been prevented through QA activities?
The same ones : if requirements/validation rules had been defined upfront via a checklist (as in Activity 4's QA example) instead of only being caught by testing after the fact.

5. How did Copilot help?
GitHub Copilot, I used to brainstorm test ideas for boundary values and invalid inputs. It quickly generated a structured list covering the constructor, Deposit, Withdraw, and CalculateTransactionFee methods, including boundary cases I hadn't initially considered — such as withdrawing the exact balance (not just more/less than it) and treating zero as a rejected input alongside negative numbers. This sped up my test planning, but the suggestions still needed review before use.

6. What Copilot suggestion did you reject or modify, and why?
I rejected the suggestion to test very large amounts (e.g. decimal.MaxValue) for overflow behavior, since that isn't a stated requirement in the lab brief — it would be testing .NET's decimal type rather than my own business logic. I also modified the suggested rounding test: it proposed checking an amount like 33.335 to confirm Math.Round behaves "as expected," but C#'s Math.Round uses banker's rounding (round-half-to-even) by default, not standard round-half-up : so I'd need to calculate the correct expected result under that rule rather than assume a naive rounding outcome.

7. What is the difference between QA and QC in this lab?
QA was writing standards/checklists and reviewing requirements before coding; 
QC was actually running the unit tests and catching the overdraft bug.
